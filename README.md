# Japan Urushi Ritual Lacquer Registry

This registry preserves traditional Japanese urushi lacquer techniques used in ritual and ceremonial craftsmanship. Urushi is one of Japan's oldest artistic practices, and many of its methods survive through specialized workshops and family lineages.

## Purpose

The goal of this contract is to protect and document essential urushi techniques in a permanent and verifiable way. Much of this knowledge is transmitted through apprenticeship, and some practices are at risk of disappearing as artisan communities shrink.

By recording selected techniques on-chain, the registry keeps a stable reference that connects each technique with its cultural and historical context.

## Why this contract matters

Urushi is not just decoration. It is a craft tied to temples, tea ceremony traditions, Noh theater, and formal Japanese aesthetics. Many of its processes are slow, precise, and deeply symbolic. Without proper documentation, these practices can be lost or misrepresented.

This registry helps by:

- preserving verified information in a neutral and permanent format  
- keeping cultural meaning attached to each technique  
- offering a reference for artisans, researchers, and cultural institutions  
- preventing the dilution of traditional knowledge  

## What makes this registry unique

The registry focuses specifically on ritual and ceremonial lacquer work. It treats the technique itself as the cultural asset, not the finished object. This approach respects how urushi artisans understand their craft: a combination of material knowledge, discipline, and cultural meaning.

The contract is intentionally minimal. It captures only the essential information needed to identify and contextualize each technique.

## How it works

Each entry includes:

- region  
- workshop  
- technique name  
- material type  
- cultural note  
- ritual or traditional use  

A simple like and dislike system allows basic community feedback.
